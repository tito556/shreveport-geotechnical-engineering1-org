Drive fifteen minutes from the sandstone bluffs of the Sunset Acres neighborhood down to the Shreveport riverfront along Clyde Fant Parkway, and you’ll notice the ground changes completely—from stiff, ancient formations to the soft, water-laden sediments of the Red River floodplain. That transition zone is where liquefaction risk lives. In our track record, engineers working on new commercial pads near the Caddo-Bossier port or infill development in the Broadmoor area often underestimate how shallow the water table sits here. The saturated, loose alluvial sands that make up much of the city’s subsurface can lose strength dramatically during a seismic event, even a moderate one. A proper soil liquefaction analysis isn’t just a box to check for the building department; it’s how we determine whether your foundation needs to be deepened, densified, or completely rethought. When the subsurface exploration flags clean sand layers below the water table, we typically recommend combining our analysis with CPT testing to capture a continuous profile of tip resistance and pore pressure, which gives us far more resolution than blow counts alone.

Liquefaction doesn't require a magnitude 9 megathrust—the duration of shaking in deep soil basins like the Red River Valley can amplify moderate distant earthquakes into a site-specific problem.

Process and scope

Shreveport sits roughly 170 miles from the New Madrid Seismic Zone, a source capable of producing magnitude 7+ events that the USGS models show would shake the Ark-La-Tex region for tens of seconds. A 2008 study by the Louisiana Geological Survey noted that the Red River alluvium—thick sequences of point-bar and channel-fill sands deposited during the Holocene—exhibits geotechnical properties remarkably similar to soils that liquefied extensively during the 2011 Christchurch earthquake. When we run a liquefaction triggering analysis here, we’re not just plugging numbers into software; we’re evaluating the cyclic stress ratio against the cyclic resistance ratio using SPT data corrected per ASTM D1586 or CPT data normalized for overburden. For projects where the fines content is uncertain, we pair the field investigation with a grain size analysis to nail down the soil behavior type index. The analysis also informs whether ground improvement is needed—and in the loose sands near Cross Lake, we’ve seen vibrocompaction turn liquefiable deposits into competent bearing material without the need for deep foundations.

Site-specific factors

The subtropical humidity of northwest Louisiana keeps the Red River alluvial aquifer perennially high, which means the saturated zone often begins just six to ten feet below the surface along the riverfront and in low-lying areas like Allendale. That’s a completely different scenario from the drier, more drained soils you’d find up on the terrace deposits near Youree Drive. The contrast matters because a high groundwater table eliminates the capillary fringe that can suppress excess pore pressure generation during shaking. When a project site sits squarely in the floodplain with a shallow water table, the liquefaction potential index can jump from marginal to high with just a slight change in the ground motion input. We’ve reviewed borings where clean sand layers at 12 feet depth would liquefy under the design earthquake, triggering settlements of three to four inches—enough to shear utility connections and crack slab-on-grade construction. The IBC requires a site-specific liquefaction study under Chapter 18 when the soil profile meets certain criteria, and skipping that step in Shreveport’s alluvial zones can lead to differential settlement that manifests years later, long after the contractor has left.

FAQ

Does Shreveport really have a liquefaction risk given the distance from major faults?

It does, and the reason is the combination of soil conditions and basin amplification. The deep sediment-filled Mississippi Embayment, which extends into northwest Louisiana, can trap and amplify seismic waves from distant sources like the New Madrid Seismic Zone. A magnitude 7.5 event there would produce long-period shaking that travels efficiently through the embayment. In the loose, saturated alluvial sands of the Red River Valley, that kind of prolonged shaking—even at moderate acceleration levels—can trigger liquefaction. The USGS hazard maps assign a non-zero PGA to Shreveport, and when you factor in a Site Class E or F profile under ASCE 7, the design ground motions often warrant a site-specific study.

What does a liquefaction analysis cost for a typical commercial site in Shreveport?

For a standard commercial lot requiring SPT borings, lab testing for fines content, and a full triggering and settlement report, the analysis typically ranges from US$2,220 to US$4,330. The final cost depends on the number of borings or CPT soundings needed, the depth of the liquefiable layers, and whether we need to run additional lab tests like Atterberg limits on selected samples. A smaller site with one or two borings and straightforward stratigraphy will fall toward the lower end, while a larger footprint requiring multiple CPT soundings and a lateral spreading assessment will be closer to the upper bound.

What's the difference between a screening-level and a detailed liquefaction analysis?

A screening-level analysis uses simplified criteria—like the Chinese criteria or the modified Seed-Idriss method—to determine whether liquefaction is even a concern at the site. It might rely on existing SPT data and basic soil descriptions to flag potentially liquefiable layers. A detailed analysis goes much further: we run the full cyclic stress ratio versus cyclic resistance ratio calculation for every sublayer, apply magnitude scaling factors, correct for overburden and fines content, and compute post-liquefaction settlements. For Shreveport projects in the alluvial plain, the IBC typically requires the detailed approach when the groundwater table is within 50 feet and the soil profile includes sands with low SPT blow counts.
