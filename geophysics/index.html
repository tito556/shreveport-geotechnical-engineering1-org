Geophysics in Shreveport encompasses a suite of non-invasive subsurface investigation methods that measure physical properties of soil, rock, and groundwater without the need for extensive drilling or excavation. These techniques are critical for understanding site conditions prior to construction, assessing geologic hazards, and complying with modern building code requirements. In a region where the subsurface can vary dramatically over short distances, geophysical surveys deliver continuous profiles that bridge the data gaps between boreholes, reducing uncertainty and helping engineers design safer, more cost-effective foundations and earthworks.

The local geology of the Shreveport area presents unique challenges that make geophysics particularly valuable. The city sits atop the Wilcox Group and alluvial deposits of the Red River floodplain, characterized by interbedded sands, silts, and clays with highly variable stiffness. Expansive clay soils are common, and paleochannels or buried sand lenses can create abrupt lateral changes in bearing capacity and seismic response. Additionally, the region's moderate seismicity — influenced by the nearby Ouachita Tectonic Belt and historic activity in the New Madrid Seismic Zone — means that characterizing dynamic soil properties is essential for critical structures. MASW and VS30 shear wave velocity profiling is the standard approach for determining site class per the International Building Code, directly measuring the stiffness of these complex sedimentary sequences.

Procedure video

National and local codes govern when and how geophysical investigations must be performed. The 2018 International Building Code (IBC), adopted by the State of Louisiana, requires Site Class determination based on the average shear wave velocity in the upper 30 meters (VS30) for Seismic Design Category C through F. This is typically achieved through MASW surveys or downhole methods. The City of Shreveport's Unified Development Code references these state-level building codes and may require geotechnical reports for commercial and subdivision developments. For environmental assessments, ASTM D6431 governs electrical resistivity and VES surveys, which are critical for mapping groundwater contamination, delineating landfill boundaries, and assessing soil corrosivity — a significant concern in the area's moist, clay-rich soils.

Projects across Shreveport routinely rely on geophysical data. Transportation infrastructure, such as the I-49 Inner City Connector and expansions at Shreveport Regional Airport, demand detailed seismic refraction and reflection tomography to map bedrock depth, identify rippability zones, and detect voids in the underlying Eocene formations. Commercial developers on Youree Drive and in the burgeoning health sciences corridor use electrical resistivity to locate buried utilities and assess groundwater conditions before excavation. Flood control levees and pump stations along the Red River require shear wave velocity data for liquefaction potential analysis, while environmental remediation sites benefit from resistivity surveys to track contaminant plumes. Even smaller-scale projects like church additions or retaining walls can benefit from geophysics when geotechnical borings alone cannot adequately characterize variable subsurface conditions.

FAQ

What is the purpose of a geophysical survey in Shreveport?

Geophysical surveys deliver non-invasive subsurface imaging to characterize soil, rock, and groundwater conditions. In Shreveport, they are used to determine seismic site class for IBC compliance, map variable alluvial soils and paleochannels, locate buried utilities, assess liquefaction potential along the Red River, and delineate contaminant plumes — all while minimizing the need for extensive drilling.

When does the building code require shear wave velocity testing in Louisiana?

The State of Louisiana adopts the IBC, which requires Site Class determination using VS30 (average shear wave velocity in the upper 30 meters) for any structure assigned to Seismic Design Category C or higher. This applies to most commercial, institutional, and essential facilities in the Shreveport area, where moderate seismicity and soft alluvial soils can amplify ground motion.

How do local soil conditions affect geophysical survey methods in Shreveport?

Shreveport's interbedded sands, silts, and expansive clays of the Wilcox Group and Red River alluvium create sharp contrasts in electrical resistivity and seismic velocity. These contrasts make methods like MASW and electrical resistivity particularly effective for mapping layer boundaries, but also demand careful array design to achieve adequate resolution and depth penetration in conductive, clay-rich near-surface soils.

What types of projects typically require geophysical investigations in the Shreveport area?

Large infrastructure projects like the I-49 Connector, airport expansions, and Red River levee improvements routinely require geophysics for bedrock mapping and liquefaction studies. Commercial developments, hospitals, schools, and industrial facilities need VS30 profiling for seismic code compliance, while environmental sites use resistivity surveys for groundwater and contamination assessment.
