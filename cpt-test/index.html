Shreveport grew fast along the Red River in the 1830s. The river deposits left behind a maze of clay, silt, and loose sand layers. These hidden transitions cause differential settlement. A standard SPT can miss thin soft seams. The CPT cone senses them instantly. Our team runs ASTM D5778 electric cone tests across Caddo Parish to map stratigraphy without gaps. For sites near Cross Lake or the river where sand layers dominate, we combine CPT data with liquefaction screening to check seismic response. For deep bridge or tower loads we pair it with pile design analysis using tip resistance and sleeve friction directly from the cone. Every test gets calibrated before and after the push. We use 15 cm² cones with 2 cm/s penetration rate. Data streams live to the operator screen. No cuttings. No disturbance. Just a continuous log of soil behavior.

One CPT push gives you tip resistance, sleeve friction, and pore pressure every centimeter. No gaps. No sample disturbance.

Process and scope

Shreveport sits at 161 feet elevation with about 187,000 people. The city's subsurface alternates between Pleistocene terrace deposits and recent alluvium. This variation kills uniform foundation assumptions. The CPT rig pushes a cone at 20 mm/s. It records tip resistance (qc), sleeve friction (fs), and pore pressure (u2) every centimeter. Friction ratio flags clay vs sand instantly. A single push can reach 60-100 feet in under an hour. That is 5x faster than a Standard Penetration Test borehole. For slab-on-grade work where fill compaction matters, we verify density with sand cone testing before the CPT run. For sites showing soft clay to depth, we assess settlement potential with mat foundation design parameters derived from cone data. The digital record eliminates transcription errors. You get a PDF log and a CSV file ready for software like CPeT-IT. We also monitor pore pressure dissipation to estimate clay consolidation rate on site.

Site-specific factors

The CPT rig is a 20-ton truck with hydraulic rams and leveling jacks. It sits directly over the push point. The operator zeroes the load cell and starts the push. If the cone hits a dense sand lens or a gravel pocket, sleeve friction spikes. The rig's reaction weight must exceed the total push force or the rods buckle. In Shreveport's alluvium, we sometimes encounter buried tree trunks or old revetment debris near the river. That stops the cone dead. We then pre-drill through the obstruction and continue. Pore pressure dissipation tests require the rig to hold position for 30-60 minutes. That demands stable ground and no nearby traffic vibration. We always scan for underground utilities before setting up. Pushing a cone into a gas line is catastrophic. Zero incidents in our record.

FAQ

How deep can a CPT go in Shreveport?

Most pushes reach 60 to 80 feet in typical alluvial soils. With a 20-ton reaction rig and dense sand refusal, we can sometimes hit 100 feet. Depth depends on soil resistance. The cone stops when tip stress exceeds 50 MPa or when we hit gravel, hardpan, or an obstruction.

What does a CPT test cost in Shreveport?

A single CPT sounding in the Shreveport area ranges from US$140 to US$260. Mobilization is separate and depends on distance. Seismic CPT add-ons and pore pressure dissipation tests increase the per-sounding cost. We deliver a firm quote after reviewing the site location and number of soundings needed.

Can CPT replace soil borings completely?

No. CPT gives continuous strength and stratigraphy data but does not recover soil samples. You still need at least one boring per site to confirm soil type visually and run lab tests like Atterberg limits and grain size analysis. We use CPT to fill the gaps between borings, reducing the total number of drill holes.

How fast do we get the CPT results?

The field data is digital and ready immediately. We deliver a draft PDF log and raw CSV file within 24 to 48 hours of completing the field work. Final signed reports with soil behavior type interpretation follow shortly after.
